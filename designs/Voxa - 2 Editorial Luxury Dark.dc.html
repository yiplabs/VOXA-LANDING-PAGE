A voice in the machine. Yours.

Voxa lives in a small glowing orb on your desktop. Tap it and talk; it answers in realtime — interruptible mid-sentence — and it can call tools. Your keys, your config, your notes: all of it stays on your machine.

№ 03The local brain

Notes that answer back.

Voxa searches, reads, and saves a folder of plain Markdown notes by voice — offline, no API key. Point it at an Obsidian vault and it talks to the notes you already keep. They remain what they always were: files on disk.

You
“Remember the standup moved to 10am.”
Voxa
“Noted.”
— later —
You
“What time is standup?”
Voxa
“Standup’s at 10.”
№ 04The Forge — self-extension

It builds its own connectors. By voice.

Say “build me a connector for the OpenWeather API” and the forge writes one — from a declarative, data-only HTTP spec, guarded against SSRF. It never executes generated code; there is no eval anywhere in the path. Tap the orb to reload, and the new tools are live.

spec — declarative HTTP, data only
validate — schema + SSRF guard
write — connectors/openweather/index.mjs
// tools live on next session. no eval, ever.

№ 07 — A short statement on privacy

No account. No telemetry. No backend. Your keys stay in a local file. Your notes are plain Markdown on disk. Privacy here is not a policy — it is the absence of anywhere else for your data to go.

Private by design · fast by habit · useful before it is impressive
№ 08 — Begin

Two commands, then talk.

Rust stable and Node 18. Paste a free Gemini API key when prompted — stored locally — allow the microphone, and say something.
